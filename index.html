Occam Razor Approach to Hedonic Pricing Theory

Occam Razor suggests that the best model is the one that retains a parameter because it has a significant effect on the fitness of the model and the removal of which increases variance. In the words of Isaac Newton "We are to admit no more causes of natural things than such as are both true and sufficient to explain their appearances."

We demonstrated that this is the best approach to hedonic pricing. In the light of the present criticism of p value as a reliable statistical tool, then most work on implicit pricing using hedonic regression confronts a serious debacle. Literature is full of earlier works validated using only p value.

We argue that since proper interpretation of the set of implicit prices in a hedonic regression determines the utility of a commodity.

Therefore, using unreliable instrument is counterproductive and defeats the theory of hedonic pricing. Going forward, we do not discard the use of p value, however, this study focuses on an Occam's Razor approach of parsimony and plurality in the analysis and investigation of the hedonic pricing econometric concept.

A Granger Causality - Machine Learning Model

We designed, developed and demonstrated using machine learning approach the impact of foreclosure sales on changes in standard sales. Our experiment was conducted using time series regression, correlation and Granger causality. Stationarity test for the experiment was based on Augmented Dickey-Fuller, Durbin-Watson and Portmanteau tests. The experiment suggests that there is a strong evidence of correlation relationship between foreclosure sales and house values. Therefore, we do not have evidence to reject the null hypothesis. However, our experiment did not establish any evidence of causality relationship between the two series

A Deep Learning Explanation of Why the Infant Cried

The goal of this project is to design, develop and evaluate a deep learning predictive model to suggest the reason as to why an infant cried. Infants express their needs through the physiological response to an unfavorable condition or environment. An infant may cry because of hunger, sleep, wet diaper, or pain. It may also be because of pathological conditions due to asphyxia, deafness or autism. Experienced nursing mothers or care givers have been trained over the years on how to interpret these reactions. However, we find it an interesting topic to explore the pattern of the cries and the facial expressions of an infant using learning algorithms. This will help inexperienced mothers and pediatricians to provide adequate and efficient care for the infant child.

The goal of this work is using deep learning approach to explore these repositories and extract useful information and patterns. Since the syntax nature of source code are very important, obviously, the NLTK which is very useful in NLP has some limitations.   3. Plagiarism detection
  4. Copyright investigation

A Cryptosystem Protocol for Predictive Models in the Cloud

In the recent time, the use of cloud computing has been popular all over the world. There are lots of benefits associated with the issue of this modern technology, however, there is a continued concern from the public about the security of information. The goal of this project is to develop a model whereby if Alice requests for computation of encrypted messages E(M_1)….E(M_n), from Bob. He computes the ciphertext using the function F{E(M1),…E(M_n )}. After the completion of the task, Bob sends an un-decrypted- computed message back to Alice.

Homomorphism provides two ways to solve the same problem and arriving at the same answer. Given two ciphertext values x and y, homomorphic algorithms provide a means whereby arithmetic operation on the plaintext produces the same result as the arithmetic operation on the ciphertext. As part of this project, we will develop an algorithm with practical implementation to e-voting.

A Machine Learning spatio-temporal model

This work focuses on an algorithmic investigation of the housing market spanning 11 years using the hedonic pricing theory. Hedonic pricing theory is an econometric concept that explains the market value of a differentiated commodity using implicit pricing.

One of the major criticisms of hedonic pricing methodology is its lack of specificity on the number of characteristic features. This is a major concern mostly when dataset span several years.

Dimensions of dataset may be large because of too many geographical submarket variables in an area under investigation.  Grouping housing submarkets into smaller clusters has a considerable impact on the size of our dataset without a significant reduction in the performance of the model.

A Machine Learning Approach to Epileptic Seizure Prediction using Electroencephalogram (EEG) Signal

This study investigates the properties of the brain electrical activity from different recording regions and physiological states for seizure detection. Neurophysiologists will find the work useful in the timely and accurate detection of epileptic seizures of their patients. We explored the best way to detect meaningful patterns from an epileptic Electroencephalogram (EEG). Signals used in this work are 23.6 second segments of 100 single channel surface EEG recordings collected with the sampling rate of 173.61 Hz.

The recorded signals are from five healthy volunteers with eyes closed and eyes open, and intracranial EEG recordings from five epilepsy patients during the seizure-free interval as well as epileptic seizures. Feature engineering was done using; i) feature extraction of each EEG wave in time, frequency and time-frequency domains via Butterworth filter, Fourier Transform and Wavelet Transform respectively and, ii) feature selection with T-test, and Sequential Forward Floating Selection (SFFS). SVM and KNN learning algorithms were applied to classify preprocessed EEG signal. Performance comparison was based on Accuracy, Sensitivity and Specificity. Our experiments showed that SVM has a slight edge over KNN.

A Multicriteria and POC Diagnostic Imaging of COVID-19 as Independent Indicators of Unfavorable Outcomes

This project involves the development of a point-of-care (POC) predictive model to estimate the risk-factors influencing unfavorable outcomes (i.e., invasive mechanical ventilation and death) of African American COVID-19 patients. Suppose the weights of \(~multicriteria~\) factors and \(~\textit{intensity ranges}~\) are defined as follows: \(\textit{v}_{i} \) is the weight associated with each \(\textit{i}-th\) criterion; \(\textit{w}_{i,j}\) is the weight associated with each \(\textit{j}-th\) sub-criterion related to the \(\textit{i}-th\) criterion; and an intensity value \(\textit{p}_{i,j}\) (normalized to 1) represents the subset of the dataset acquired about the AA COVID-19 for the \(\textit{i}-th\) criterion and the \(\textit{j} -th\) sub-criterion. For instance, if the \(\textit{Symptoms}\) criterion (\textit{i} = 1)\) and the sub-criterion \(\textit{Fever} (\textit{j} = 1)\) are considered, \(\textit{p}_{1,1 }\) represents the number of AA COVID-19 patients with a fever divided by the total number of AA COVID-19 patients. An equation is defined to evaluate the risk of AA COVID-19 patients for unfavorable outcomes by aggregating the defined weights as: \begin{equation} \tag{1} R_{\textit{COVID}-19}=v_{1}\left(\sum _{j=1}^{M}w_{i,j}p_{1,j}\right)\cdot v_{2}\left(\sum _{j=1}^{N}w_{2,j}\cdot p_{2,j}\right)\cdot v_{3}\left(\sum _{j=1}^{P}w_{3,j}\cdot p_{3,j}\right). v_{4}\left(\sum _{j=1}^{Q}w_{4,j} .p_{4,j}\right) \end{equation} Therefore, it is possible to define the function \(F(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w})\) , which evaluates the difference between the values of risk \(\mathbf{R}_{\textit{COVID}-19}^{l}(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w})\) and the effective unfavorable outcomes \(\phi _{\textit{Infection}}^{l}\) registered in the counties within an area of interest, e.g. Washington DC metro area The figure below illustrates the multicriteria method and POC diagnostic imaging as independent indicators of unfavorable outcomes, as well as combining the two to construct a classifier to identify AA COVID-19 patients who are at-risk for experiencing unfavorable outcomes. The significant risk factors identified in the multicriteria method are fed into the classifier, in addition to the CNN model of POC diagnostic images.


Deep Neural Networks for Human's Fall-risk Prediction using Force-Plate Time Series Signal

Early and accurate identification of the balance deficits could reduce falls, in particular for older adults, a prone population. Our work investigates deep neural networks' capacity to identify human balance patterns towards predicting fall-risk. Human balance ability can be characterized based on commonly used balance metrics, such as those derived from the force-plate time series. We hypothesized that low, moderate, and high risk of falling can be characterized based on balance metrics, derived from the force-plate time series, in conjunction with deep learning algorithms. Further, we predicted that our proposed One-One-One Deep Neural Networks algorithm provides a considerable increase in performance compared to other algorithms. Here, an open-source force-plate dataset, which quantified human balance from a wide demographic of human participants (163 females and males aged 18-86) for varied standing conditions (eyes-open firm surface, eyes-closed firm surface, eyes-open foam surface, eyes-closed foam surface) was used. Classification was based on one of the several indicators of fall-risk tied to the fear of falling: the clinically used Falls Efficacy Scale (FES) assessment. For human fall-risk prediction, the deep learning architecture implemented comprised of: Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), Long-Short Time Memory (LSTM), One Dimensional Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN), and a proposed One-One-One Deep Neural Network. Results showed that our One-One-One Deep Neural Networks algorithm outperformed the other aforementioned algorithms and state-of-the-art models on the same dataset. With an accuracy, precision, and sensitivity of 99.9%, 100%, 100%, respectively at the 12th epoch, we found that our proposed One-One-One Deep Neural Network model is the most efficient neural network in predicting human's fall-risk (based on the FES measure) using the force-plate time series signal. This is a novel methodology for an accurate prediction of human risk of fall.

COVID-19 County Level Severity Classification with Imbalance Class Dataset: A NearMiss Under-sampling Approach

Abstract — COVID-19 pandemic that broke out in the late 2019 has spread across the globe. The disease has infected millions of people. Thousands of lives have been lost. The momentum of the disease has been slowed by the introduction of vaccine. However, some countries are still recording high number of casualties. The focus of this work is to design, develop and evaluate a machine learning county level COVID-19 severity classifier. The proposed model will predict severity of the disease in a county into low, moderate, or high. Policy makers will find the work useful in the distribution of vaccines. Five learning algorithms were trained and evaluated. Class imbalance was addressed using under-sampling of the majority classes. The result of our experiment shows that the ensemble models outperformed the non-ensemble models by a considerable margin.

COVID-19 Fatality Rate Classification using Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) for Imbalance Class

Abstract—SARS-Cov-2 is not to be introduced anymore. The global pandemic that originated more than a year ago in Wuhan, China has claimed thousands of lives. Since the arrival of this plague, face mask has become part of our dressing code. The focus of this study is to design, develop and evaluate a COVID-19 fatality rate classifier at the county level. The proposed model predicts fatality rate as low, moderate, or high. This will help government and decision makers to improve mitigation strategy and provide measures to reduce the spread of the disease. Tourists and travelers will also find the work useful in planning of trips. Dataset used in the experiment contained imbalanced fatality levels. Therefore, class imbalance was offset using SMOTE. Evaluation of the proposed model was based on precision, F1 score, accuracy, and ROC curve. Five learning algorithms were trained and evaluated. Experimental results showed the Bagging model has the best performance.

Automatic Electrocardiogram Detection of Suspected Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy: Application to Wearable Heart Monitors

LVH patient processed ECG signal with automatically detected ECG features: QRS complex, TOnset, TPeak, and TOffset.

Abstract—In this work, an automatic detection algorithm for hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is presented. Of particular interest is the algorithm's ability to differentiate HCM subjects and healthy volunteers from a single lead ECG dataset. Suspected HCM subjects are identified by the primary clinical abnormality associated with HCM, left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH). In total, n=43 human subjects ECG datasets are investigated: n=21 healthy volunteers and n=22 left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) patients. Significant differences of p-value 0.01 and 0.04 were found for the respective ECG parameters, S-wave amplitude and ST-segment, when differentiating between the LVH patients and healthy human volunteers.

A CNN Transfer Learning - Electrocardiogram (ECG) Signal Approach to Predict COVID-19

Abstract — Deep learning is increasingly gaining traction in cutting-edge medical sciences such as image classification, and genomics due to the high computational performance and accuracy in evaluating medical data. In this study, we investigate the cardiac properties of ECG Images and predict COVID-19 in a binary classification of patients who tested positive for COVID-19 and Normal Persons who tested negative. We analyzed the electrocardiogram (ECG) images by preprocessing the ECG data and building an ECG- Deep Learning- COVID-19 (ECG-DL-COVID) classifiers to predict disease. The deep learning models in our experiments constituted CNN, Multi -Layer Perceptron (MLP), and Transfer Learning. Performance evaluation was done to compare the effectiveness of the proposed methodologies with other COVID-19 deep learning related works. In the three experiments, we achieved an 87% prediction accuracy for MLP, a 90% prediction for CNN and a 93.8% prediction for Transfer Learning. Experimental results and performance evaluation show that the proposed models outperformed previous deep learning models in the prediction of COVID-19 by a considerable margin.

Deep Neural Network and NRCLexicon Classification of Sentiments with Emotional Reactions from COVID-19 Vaccination Tweets

Abstract — Popular social platforms like Twitter have become a reliable source of information with their swift information dissemination. Individuals with different backgrounds convey their opinions through social media platforms. Therefore, analyzing datasets from social media, like Twitter, can offer various perspectives on important subject like COVID-19 vaccination. Public health organizations can identify factors contributing to vaccine assurance based on chronological data. In this study, public tweets were downloaded daily using the Tweeter API. Before performing text sentiment computation, tweets were cleansed and normalized. Neural network learning algorithms: 1DCNN, LSTM, Dense layer, and BERT were trained and evaluated. Classification was based on positive, negative, joy, sadness, trust, disgust, fear, anger, surprise, and anticipation (10 classes). Our study shows that there is significant evidence that tweeter users tend to express positive sentiments about the Covid-19 vaccine. People trust that Covid-19 is a real issue, not a hoax and its vaccine are an effective emergency treatment to combat the pandemic. Furthermore, societies can hold their anger when coping with the novel virus and its vaccinations. Our experiment also shows that BERT model performs the best in this research study with an accuracy of 0.9671 (using ten classes) with training times of 8,429 seconds.

Early Detection of Silent Oxygen Deficiency in Elderly Adults


Wrist-worn single-lead ECG monitor

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), nursing homes are at high risk for infection and death from the novel coronavirus (COVID-19). In the United States, more than 143,000 elderly-care facility residents have been infected with the novel COVID-19. Within this population, it is estimated that 25,600 have died, accounting for a third of the country's pandemic fatalities. Most COVID-19 patients were hypoxic or hypoxemic (SaO2 <90%) before intubation and during intubation. Additionally, many patients that go on to develop respiratory failure had hypoxemia but without any signs of respiratory distress, this is referred to as "silent hypoxemia". Also, patients have demonstrated silent hypoxia. Hypoxia and hypoxemia describe states of oxygen deficiency: hypoxia is deficiency in oxygenation at tissue or cellular level, while hypoxemia is a suboptimal normal partial pressure of oxygen. Hypoxia and hypoxemia are interrelated, as untreated hypoxemia will ultimately result in hypoxia. In this project, we propose to investigate the early detection of silent oxygen deficiency as an indicator of COVID-19 infection, in order to reduce the spread of COVID-19 in the elderly care living facilities.

The ultimate goal of this project is to determine the feasibility of detecting silent hypoxia/hypoxemia from the combined electrocardiogram (ECG) and respiratory rate (RR) parameters measured from a wearable sensor. The hypoxia detection algorithm utilize data obtain from a wearable device. The wearable device is equipped with a single-lead ECG sensor and tri-axial accelerometers. To obtain a respiratory signal from the tri-axial accelerometer measurements, a single, time varying and acceleration (linear or angular) vector is derived.

A Machine Learning - Sentiment Analysis on Monkeypox Outbreak: An Extensive Dataset to Show the Polarity of Public Opinion from Twitter Tweets.

Research on sentiment analysis has proven to be very useful in public health, particularly in analyzing infectious diseases. As the world recovers from the onslaught of the covid-19 pandemic, concerns are rising that another pandemic, known as monkeypox, might hit the world again. Monkeypox is an infectious disease whose cases have been confirmed and reported in over 73 countries across the globe. The sporadic virus outbreak has been a significant concern for many individuals and health authorities. Therefore, seeking information, guidelines, and health protocols related to this virus becomes critical. This study aims to analyze public views on monkeypox tweets on social media. Decision-makers will find the work helpful in crafting policies to control the spread of the disease. In this study, we collected over 500,000 tweets related to the monkeypox on Twitter. The dataset was annotated using Vader and TextBlob to label data into positive, negative, and neutral sentiments. Stemming and Lemmatization techniques were deployed to normalize the tokens. Vectorization was based on CountVectorizer and TF-IDF methodologies. We designed, developed, and evaluated 56 models. K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), Naïve Bayes, and XGBoost were deployed as learning algorithms. Performance Evaluation was based on accuracy, F1 Score, Precision, and Recall. Our experimental result showed that TextBlob annotation + Lemmatization + CountVectorizer + SVM model yielded the highest accuracy of about 0.9348. Overall, this study establishes the need for an active public health presence to guide against the spread of negative sentiment about the monkeypox virus on social media platforms.

Text Mining and Emotion Classification on Monkeypox Twitter Dataset: A Deep Learning-Natural Language Processing (NLP) Approach

Emotion classification has become a valuable tool in analyzing text and emotions people express in response to events or crises, particularly on social media and other online platforms. The recent news about monkeypox highlighted various emotions individuals felt during the outbreak. People's opinions and concerns have been very different based on their awareness and understanding of the disease. Although there have been studies on monkeypox, emotion classification related to this virus has not been considered. As a result, this study aims to analyze the emotions individual expressed on social media posts related to the monkeypox disease. Our goal is to provide real-time information and identify critical concerns about the disease. To conduct our analysis, first, we extract and preprocess 800,000 datasets and then use NRCLexicon, a Python library, to predict and measure the emotional significance of each text. Secondly, we develop deep learning models based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Bi- directional LSTM (BiLSTM), and the combination of Convolutional Neural Networks and Long Short-Term Memory (CLSTM) for emotion classification. We use SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) and Random Undersampling techniques to address the class imbalance in our training dataset. The results of our study revealed that the CNN model achieved the highest performance with an accuracy of 96%. Overall, emotion classification on the monkeypox dataset can be a powerful tool for improving our understanding of the disease.
